# Email Craft — Patterns, Frameworks, and Examples

> Read copy-style-guide.md first. This document is the tactical layer — how to actually build converting emails.
>
> Everything here assumes: No em dashes. One idea per email. Under 200 words. Open with the hook, never "Hey {{first_name}}!"

## THE THREE EMAIL ARCHETYPES

Every converting email is one of three types. Not four. Not seven. Three.

When you're about to write an email, pick the archetype first. Then write.

### 1. THE STORY EMAIL

A specific story about a specific person, with a specific lesson the reader needs.

**Structure:**
- Open with the scene ("A guy reached out last month. 760 score. Solid income.")
- Let the story unfold with concrete details (dollar amounts, timelines, names)
- Land the lesson in one line ("Same credit. Same banks. Different sequence. Completely different outcome.")
- Bridge to the reader's situation
- Soft CTA

**When to use:** Opt-in welcome, early nurture, proof-building, reactivation.

**What makes it work:** Stories bypass skepticism. A claim gets analyzed. A story gets felt. Then the lesson lands after the feeling.

**What kills it:** Generic stories. "A client of ours once" without specifics. If you don't have a real client story, get one from the offer doc or mine the transcripts. If there's nothing specific, use a different archetype.

### 2. THE INSIGHT EMAIL

A counter-intuitive or under-known fact about their world. Pure value, no selling.

**Structure:**
- Open with the insight as a statement ("Most people don't know this.")
- Unpack the mechanism in 2-3 short paragraphs
- Explain what it means for them specifically
- Make the ask feel like the obvious next step

**When to use:** Authority-building phase, when the list is going cold, when you need to deliver value before asking again.

**What makes it work:** It teaches. The reader feels smarter for having read it. That credit transfers to you.

**What kills it:** Insights that are obvious. "Good follow-up is important." If the reader already knew it, they delete. The insight must be something they haven't heard, framed in a way they haven't seen.

### 3. THE DIRECT ASK EMAIL

No story. No insight. Just a clean, honest ask.

**Structure:**
- Open with the current state ("I've been sending you emails for a few weeks now.")
- State the ask clearly
- Offer the easy exit (dignity for the no)
- Single CTA

**When to use:** Last push before transitioning to long-term nurture. When someone's gone quiet and you need a yes-or-no.

**What makes it work:** Fatigue is a conversion tool. When the reader's been passive for weeks, asking directly cuts through. "If you're still serious, let's talk. If not, no hard feelings" respects their time.

**What kills it:** Desperate energy. "PLEASE reply." "Last chance!!" If the ask feels needy, the reader punishes you. Direct means confident, not anxious.

## SUBJECT LINE FRAMEWORKS (the 6 that actually work)

Subject lines do 80% of the work. Open rate is the only metric that matters at this stage.

### 1. The Specific Number
"The $47K mistake nobody talks about"
"47 missed calls last month"
"3 hard inquiries is the line"

Why: Numbers feel real. "A big mistake" feels like marketing. "$47K" feels like a story.

### 2. The Unfinished Thought
"Before you apply anywhere, read this"
"One thing you need to know first"
"Your credit is a ticking clock"

Why: Opens a loop. The brain needs to close loops. They open to close it.

### 3. The Counter-Intuitive Claim
"You don't have a funding problem"
"You're not bad with money"
"Why we charge upfront"

Why: Contradicts what they expect. The "wait, what?" reaction drives the open.

### 4. The Character or Client Name
"Ashley got $100K. Here's how."
"The daycare owner who almost quit"
"The entrepreneur who funded his franchise"

Why: Names create curiosity. They read because they want to know what happened to a specific person.

### 5. The Question That Must Be Answered
"Still thinking about it?"
"Know anyone else looking for funding?"
"Did you give up?"

Why: Questions trigger response. Used sparingly — one-question subjects lose power if repeated.

### 6. The First Name Only
"{{first_name}}?"
"{{first_name}}"
"It's been a year, {{first_name}}"

Why: The highest-open subject of any type. Feels personal. Don't overuse — once per nurture cycle max, or the pattern breaks.

### Subject line rules:
- Under 50 characters. Mobile cuts off.
- Lowercase where natural ("quick thought" vs "Quick Thought").
- Never clickbait. The subject must deliver on what the email says.
- No em dashes. No "..." ellipses on every line.
- No corporate subjects. "Monthly newsletter" is dead on arrival.

## THE FIRST LINE (the preheader)

After the subject, the first line is what gets previewed in the inbox. It must earn the open a second time.

**Rules:**
- Never "Hi {{first_name}}! I hope this email finds you well."
- Open with a scene, a number, a claim, or a confession.
- Must feel like the email is already mid-conversation.

**Good first lines (study these):**

> "A guy reached out to us last month. Great credit. 760 score. Solid income."

> "Here's something most funding companies won't tell you."

> "Got a call last week from a woman who was about to give up on her daycare business."

> "Most people don't know this."

> "I've been sending you emails for a few weeks now."

> "{{first_name}}, random question."

**Bad first lines (never do this):**

> "Hope you're having a great week!"

> "I wanted to reach out and share..."

> "We're excited to announce..."

> "As we approach the end of Q3..."

> "In today's competitive market..."

## PARAGRAPH PACING (The Rolling Reveal applied to email)

Each paragraph advances the reveal by one step. No backtracking. No summarizing.

**Bad pacing (AI default):**
> "There's a problem in funding. Many people don't know about it. It's called the shotgun approach. The shotgun approach means applying to many banks at once. This creates hard inquiries. Hard inquiries hurt your credit."

Every sentence restates the previous one. Deadly.

**Good pacing:**
> "Most people don't know this.
>
> If you have more than three hard inquiries within a six month period, your funding potential drops significantly. Banks see it as a red flag.
>
> The shotgun approach guarantees you blow past that number. Our approach keeps you under it.
>
> That's the difference between $30K in approvals and $130K."

Four paragraphs. Four distinct reveals. Each builds on the last without repeating it. By paragraph four, the conclusion lands with full weight.

### White space is a weapon.

One-line paragraphs exist.

Use them.

Then go back to a longer paragraph that earns its length by delivering something substantial. Then another one-line punch.

Paragraphs are rhythm. Treat them that way.

## THE EARNED CTA

The CTA never pitches. The copy above it pitched. The CTA just makes the next step obvious.

**Bad CTA (desperate):**
> "Click here NOW to book your FREE consultation before time runs out!"

**Good CTA (earned):**
> "Grab a time here: {{booking_link}}"

**Good CTA (slightly warmer):**
> "Your call: {{booking_link}}"

**Good CTA (for direct-ask emails):**
> "If you're still serious, {{booking_link}}. If not, no hard feelings."

**Good CTA (casual reply-based):**
> "Just reply 'yes' and I'll send you the details."

If the reader doesn't already want the next step by the time they get to the CTA, the copy above it failed. Don't fix the CTA. Fix the copy above it.

**Why it works:** Specific person, specific numbers ($47K, $120K, 760 score, 6 banks). The lesson lands in one line: "Same credit. Same banks. Different sequence." The CTA is the obvious next step by the time you get there.

### Example 2: Insight Email (Authority)
**Subject:** 3 hard inquiries is the line
```
Most people don't know this.

If you have more than three hard inquiries within a six month period, your funding potential drops significantly. Banks see it as a red flag.

{{booking_link}}

Blake
```

**Why it works:** Opens with the insight as a statement. Unpacks the mechanism (3 inquiries = red flag). Quantifies the stakes ($30K vs $130K). The CTA is a single link — the copy already did the work.

### Example 3: Story Email (Social Proof)
**Subject:** Ashley got $100K. Here's how.
```
We had a client named Ashley who came to us after trying to get funding on her own.

She'd watched the YouTube videos. Hired another funding company. They used what we call the shotgun approach. Applied her everywhere, didn't care about sequence or timing, and left her with a pile of hard inquiries and low approvals.

Blake
```

**Why it works:** Named character (Ashley). Specific backstory (YouTube videos, another funding company). Concrete outcome ($100K). The counterfactual ("If she'd come to us first") makes the reader imagine themselves in a better version. Lesson lands in one clean line.

### Example 4: Insight Email (Identity Shift)
**Subject:** You don't have a funding problem
```
Here's what I've learned after helping hundreds of entrepreneurs get funded.

The ones who struggle don't have a credit problem. They have a strategy problem.

They apply to banks randomly. They don't know which lenders to hit first. They don't understand how timing between applications affects approvals. And by the time they figure it out, they've already burned through their best opportunities.

You don't need better credit. You need better orchestration.

That's literally what we do. We take your profile, map the exact sequence, and control the entire process so you walk away with the maximum amount possible.

If your credit is 700+ and you haven't started applying yet, you're in the best position you'll ever be in. Let's not waste it.

{{booking_link}}

Blake
```

**Why it works:** Reframes the problem (not credit, strategy). The reframe IS the insight. Specific and concrete ("which lenders to hit first," "timing between applications"). The CTA is earned — by paragraph four, the reader wants the orchestration.

### Example 5: Authority Email (Price Reframe)
**Subject:** Why we charge upfront
```
Some funding companies charge nothing upfront. Sounds great, right?

Here's the problem with that model.

When there's no skin in the game, the company doesn't invest in talent. They hire cheap reps who use the shotgun approach. Apply everywhere, pray for approvals, and move on to the next client.

We charge $7,500 upfront because that's what it costs to hire people who actually know what they're doing. Our senior account managers make six figures. They know which banks to hit, which order to apply, and how to protect your credit throughout the process.

The result? Our clients average $50K to $150K per round across three rounds. Not because we're lucky. Because we're strategic.

If you're comparing us to the "free upfront" companies, you're comparing a surgeon to a guy with a YouTube tutorial and a sharp knife.

Your call: {{booking_link}}

Blake
```

**Why it works:** Takes the hardest objection (price) and turns it into proof. The "surgeon vs. YouTube tutorial" analogy lands the whole argument in one image. Specific salaries, specific ranges, specific process.

### Example 6: Direct Ask Email
**Subject:** Still thinking about it?
```
{{first_name}}, I've been sending you emails for a few weeks now.

Not to be pushy. Just because I know what happens when people sit on a good credit profile without using it strategically.

Nothing. Nothing happens. The credit stays the same. The business stays the same. The funding potential slowly erodes as time passes.

If you're still serious about getting funded, the window is now. If not, no hard feelings.

{{booking_link}}

Blake
```

**Why it works:** Acknowledges the situation (several emails, no response). The "Nothing. Nothing happens." repetition is rhythm at work — it mimics the stagnation. Offers the easy exit. Clean ask.

## ANGLE BANK (for the Email Library)

When generating emails for a journey-stage client, use these six angles as the library organization. Each angle is a category of persuasion. Rotate through them.

### 1. Pain + Loss Aversion
Core idea: What the reader is losing by staying still.
Example subject lines:
- "The [specific dollar] mistake nobody talks about"
- "Your [asset/profile/window] is a ticking clock"
- "[Specific threshold] is the line"

### 2. Authority
Core idea: Why trust the sender over alternatives.
Example subject lines:
- "Why we [unusual practice]"
- "What [surprising authority source] actually looks at"
- "What the [alternative competitors] won't tell you"

### 3. Social Proof
Core idea: Specific real people got specific real results.
Example subject lines:
- "[Name] got [result]. Here's how."
- "The [character descriptor] who almost quit"
- "How [name] went from [before] to [after]"

### 4. Identity Shift
Core idea: The problem isn't what the reader thinks it is.
Example subject lines:
- "You don't have a [surface problem] problem"
- "You're not bad with [thing]"
- "[Asset they underestimate] is a weapon"

### 5. Direct Ask
Core idea: Clean yes-or-no ask, easy exit.
Example subject lines:
- "Still thinking about it?"
- "{{first_name}}?"
- "Don't [action] if you're not serious"

### 6. Seasonal / Cyclical
Core idea: A time-based reason to act now.
Example subject lines:
- "New quarter, new numbers"
- "The [season] [opportunity] window"
- "The [time-based] rule"

## THE TIME-BASED 52-WEEK NURTURE (for time-based clients)

If the client uses a time-based nurture (calendar-driven), structure the 52 weeks around phases that mirror the lead's journey:

### Phase 1: Days 0-14 / Weeks 1-2 — Convert Hot Leads
- Frequency: Daily email + daily SMS
- Tone: Urgent but not desperate. They just raised their hand. Strike while hot.
- Archetypes: Story (pain) → Insight (mechanism) → Social proof → Identity shift → Direct ask
- Angles to lead with: Pain + Loss Aversion, Cost of Inaction, Speed

### Phase 2: Weeks 3-6 — Convert Warm Leads
- Frequency: 3 emails per week
- Tone: Story-driven. Build relationship. Show you understand their world.
- Archetypes: Mostly Story + Insight
- Angles to lead with: Authority, Social Proof, Identity Shift

### Phase 3: Weeks 7-12 — Revive Dormant
- Frequency: 2 emails per week
- Tone: "Still thinking?" Direct but not pushy. Case studies land here.
- Archetypes: Insight + Direct Ask
- Angles: Cost of Inaction, Price Reframe, Direct Ask

### Phase 4: Weeks 13-26 — Belief Building
- Frequency: Weekly
- Tone: Thought leadership. Teach without giving away everything.
- Archetypes: Insight + Story
- Angles: Authority, Identity Shift, rotating

### Phase 5: Weeks 27-52 — Stay Top of Mind
- Frequency: Weekly
- Tone: Casual. Mix of proof and seasonal.
- Archetypes: Story + Seasonal
- Angles: Rotate all six

### Loop rule: After week 52, sequence restarts at week 1. Nobody remembers an email from 9 months ago.

## THE ANTI-EXAMPLES (never write like this)

Study these so you recognize them and kill them on sight.

### AI-written welcome email (what NOT to produce):
```
Hi {{first_name}}!

I hope this email finds you well! We are thrilled to welcome you to our community and are excited to embark on this journey with you.

At [COMPANY], we believe in empowering our clients to unlock their full potential through our innovative, comprehensive, and world-class solutions. Our team of industry-leading experts is committed to delivering results that exceed expectations.

We look forward to connecting with you soon to explore how we can help you transform your business and navigate the path to success!

Warm regards,
The [COMPANY] Team
```

Everything wrong with this:
- "Hope this finds you well" — AI tell
- "Thrilled to welcome you" — nobody is thrilled
- "Embark on this journey" — cliche
- "Unlock their full potential" — abstraction
- "Innovative, comprehensive, world-class" — meaningless superlatives
- "Industry-leading experts" — fake authority
- "Exceed expectations" — promises nothing specific
- "Transform your business" — vague
- "Navigate the path to success" — cliche stack
- "Warm regards" — corporate signature
- Zero specificity. Zero voice. Zero reason to keep reading.

### Fixed version (what TO produce):
```
{{first_name}}, you just signed up to learn about the TLU method. Good timing.

Here's why.

Most people who try to get business funding do it wrong. They apply to 4 or 5 banks at random, get denied at half of them, and the denials leave hard inquiries that tank their approval odds for the next 6 months.

By the time they figure out what happened, the damage is done.

That's what we prevent. We look at your full credit profile, figure out exactly which banks to apply to, in what order, and we control the timing so you get the maximum approval with the minimum damage.

The next step is a 15-minute call where we pull your profile and show you your actual number. Not a guess. Not a range. Your number.

If we can help, you'll see exactly how. If we can't, we'll tell you before you waste any time.

Grab a time here: {{booking_link}}

Blake
Baxter Funding Academy
```

Notice: Specific numbers (4-5 banks, 6 months, 15 minutes). Specific mechanism ("which banks, in what order, controlled timing"). Clear expectation ("your actual number, not a guess"). Honest framing ("if we can't, we'll tell you"). Zero corporate speak.

## THE FINAL TEST FOR EVERY EMAIL

Before sending, run through these seven checks:

1. **Is there one clear idea, stated in one sentence?** If no, rewrite.
2. **Is there a real number, name, or scene in the first paragraph?** If no, add one.
3. **Does every paragraph reveal something new?** If any paragraph summarizes a previous one, cut it.
4. **Does the subject line pass the mobile test?** (Under 50 chars, readable in a preview pane.)
5. **Would the reader read to the end because the copy earned it, not because they have to?**
6. **Is the CTA earned?** Would they naturally want the next step by the time they get to it?
7. **Did you search for and remove em dashes, "leverage," "unlock," "transform," "navigate," "seamless," "world-class," "game-changing"?**

If it passes all seven, ship.
