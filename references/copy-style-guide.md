# The Copy Craft Reference

> We are in the conversion business. Not the clever business. Not the creative business. Not the content business. Copy exists for one reason: to get the reader to take the next action.
>
> Every decision below flows from that premise. Read this document before writing a single SMS or email.

---

## THE ONE PRINCIPLE THAT UNDERPINS EVERYTHING

**Specific beats clever. Every time.**

"$47K walked out the door last month" beats "You're missing out on revenue."
"Blake called 6 times yesterday" beats "We have aggressive follow-up."
"Ashley got $100K in round one" beats "Our clients see great results."

When you're tempted to be creative, get more specific instead. Numbers. Names. Scenes. Dates. Dollar amounts. Real things.

This is the single rule that separates the pros from the amateurs. Internalize it.

---

## WHO TO WRITE LIKE (study these, don't quote them)

This skill has studied and absorbed the craft from:

- **Gary Halbert** (Boron Letters) — write to one person at the kitchen table. A-pile vs. B-pile. Proof and specificity.
- **Eugene Schwartz** (Breakthrough Advertising) — market awareness levels. Match the copy to where the reader's head is.
- **Joe Sugarman** (Adweek Copywriting Handbook) — the slippery slope. Each sentence's only job is to make you read the next.
- **David Ogilvy** — the consumer isn't a moron, she's your wife. Respect the reader. Research the product.
- **Dan Kennedy** — message-market-media match. Personality > polish. Real urgency only.
- **Stefan Georgi** (RMBC method) — Research, Mechanism, Big Idea, Copy. One big idea per piece.
- **Kyle Milligan** (Rolling Reveal) — one new reveal per paragraph. Forward motion only. No backtracking.
- **Ben Settle** (Email Players) — entertain, then sell. One idea per email. Soap opera sequences.
- **Daniel Throssell** — voice-first. The reader reads BECAUSE of the voice, not despite it.
- **Parris Lampropoulos / Ning Li** — voice-of-customer research. Mine the client's actual language.
- **Craig Clemens** (Golden Hippo) — conversation headlines. Someone just walked up and started talking.
- **Gary Bencivenga** (Bencivenga Bullets) — proof, proof, proof. Nothing converts without it.

We're not copying them. We're writing in their tradition — direct response that respects the reader and earns the conversion.

## THE CARDINAL TEST

Before any piece of copy goes out, it must pass this test:

**"If a friend sent me this text / email, would I reply? Would I read to the end? Would I think about it after?"**

Not "is it well-written." Not "does it follow the rules." Not "is it on-brand."

Would a human who got this actually respond.

If no, throw it out and start again.

---

## VOICE-OF-CUSTOMER RESEARCH (DO THIS BEFORE WRITING)

Before writing a single word for any client, mine these sources for their actual language:

1. **The Offer Doc** — What exact phrases does the client use for their pain points, their promise, their mechanism, their proof?
2. **Fireflies transcripts** (if provided) — What words does the client use on sales calls? What metaphors? What's their rhythm?
3. **Client reviews / testimonials** (if accessible) — The customers' exact words are the copy.
4. **Landing page / ads the lead saw** — What promise triggered the opt-in? The first message must reference it.

If the offer doc says "patients," every piece of copy says "patients." Not "clients." Not "customers."
If the client's testimonials say "I was drowning in leads I couldn't follow up with," that phrase goes into an email subject.
If the ad promised "see how much funding you qualify for," the first SMS references that exact promise.

**Generic copy fails because it's written in a marketer's language. Great copy fails because it's written in the customer's language.**

## THE SLIPPERY SLOPE (Sugarman)

The first job of every sentence is to make the reader read the next sentence. That's it. That's the whole job.

- **First sentence:** Short. Easy. Pulls them in with zero friction.
- **Second sentence:** Easier than the first. They're committed now.
- **Third sentence:** The reveal, the hook, the pain point, the story beat.
- **Rest of copy:** Ride the momentum you earned in the first three sentences.

If a sentence doesn't pull them forward, cut it. Ruthless.

Applied to email: the subject earns the open. The first line earns the second. The second earns the third. By paragraph two, they're gliding.

Applied to SMS: every text is a slippery slope between texts. The 3-text pattern works because text 1 earns text 2, text 2 earns text 3.

## THE BIG IDEA (Schwartz / Georgi)

Every email has ONE idea. Not three. Not "here are five ways to..." ONE.

Before writing an email, state the idea in one sentence:
- "If you apply to the wrong banks first, you burn your best chances."
- "Your credit profile today is the best profile you'll have for 12 months."
- "The shotgun approach costs you $100K."

If you can't state the idea in one sentence, don't write the email. Get clearer first.

Every line in the email must serve that idea. Every paragraph must deepen it. The CTA must be the natural next step from believing it.

**Emails fail because they try to say everything. They convert when they say one thing well.**

---

## ROLLING REVEAL (Milligan)

Each paragraph reveals one new thing. No backtracking. No summarizing. No "as I said earlier."

Paragraph 1 sets up the situation.
Paragraph 2 introduces the problem.
Paragraph 3 reveals the unexpected cause.
Paragraph 4 shows the consequence.
Paragraph 5 points to the solution.
Paragraph 6 makes the ask.

If you find yourself restating something from an earlier paragraph, you've stalled. Cut it, advance the reveal.

---

## RHYTHM

Read your copy out loud. If it sounds like a robot, it is one.

**Vary sentence length.** Short sentences. Then a longer sentence that flows for a while and lets the reader breathe. Then short. Short. Medium.

**Vary paragraph length.** One-word paragraphs exist for emphasis.

Use them.

Then drop into a longer paragraph that builds pressure before releasing into another short one.

**Match the rhythm to the emotion.** Excitement is staccato. Sadness is long flowing lines. Urgency is sharp. Intimacy is slow.

The #1 sign of AI-written copy is uniform sentence length. Every sentence 12-15 words. Every paragraph 3-4 lines. No rhythm. No breath.

## ANTI-PATTERNS (The AI Tells)

These are the instant giveaways. If your copy has any of these, rewrite.

### Vocabulary tells
- "Navigate" ("navigate this challenge")
- "Leverage" ("leverage our platform")
- "Unlock" ("unlock your potential")
- "Transform" ("transform your business")
- "Elevate" ("elevate your results")
- "Streamline" ("streamline your workflow")
- "Empower"
- "Robust"
- "Seamless"
- "Holistic"
- "Cutting-edge"
- "Best-in-class"
- "World-class"
- "Game-changing"
- "Revolutionary"
- "Innovative"
- "Comprehensive"
- "Optimize"

If a real human at a kitchen table wouldn't say it, don't write it.

### Phrasing tells
- "I hope this message finds you well" — instant delete
- "I wanted to reach out" — everyone does this, signals automation
- "Per our conversation" — nobody talks like this
- "At your earliest convenience" — burn it
- "Dear {{first_name}}" — only from bots and old companies
- "We're excited to announce" — nobody cares what you're excited about
- "In today's fast-paced world" — kill it with fire
- "Are you struggling with..." — a question as a headline is the weakest opener

### Structural tells
- **Em dashes** — the single biggest AI giveaway. Use commas and periods instead.
- **The "not just X, but Y, and Z" triple** — over-used by LLMs. Avoid.
- **Starting every email with the reader's first name** — automation signal.
- **Three-bullet lists everywhere** — real humans don't speak in bullets.
- **Perfect grammar in every message** — humans mess up. Start sentences with "and" or "but." Drop apostrophes in texts. Use fragments.
- **Balanced sentence lengths** — rhythm requires variance.
- **Hedge words** — "might," "could potentially," "may help." Direct-response copy is confident or it's dead.

### Emotion tells
- Over-claimed feelings: "You're going to LOVE this."
- Forced enthusiasm: "We're THRILLED to share..."
- Fake intimacy: "I was just thinking about you..."
- Manufactured urgency: "LAST CHANCE!!!" (when it isn't)

## HOW TO SOUND SUPERHUMAN WITHOUT SOUNDING TRYHARD

"Superhuman" copy isn't punchier. It's not more creative. It's not more emotional.

It's **more specific, more confident, more true**.

### The Superhuman Shift (do all three):

**1. Replace abstractions with scenes.**
- Bad: "Your follow-up is costing you money."
- Good: "Yesterday a homeowner submitted your form at 2:14pm. By 2:51pm they'd called three of your competitors. You called back at 4:30."

**2. Replace claims with proof.**
- Bad: "We get great results."
- Good: "Our last HVAC client did $44 per qualified lead last week. Long Island, $5K/month spend, 287 booked calls."

**3. Replace the sell with a fact.**
- Bad: "Book a call to learn more!"
- Good: "15 minutes. We pull your profile, show you your number. If we can't help, I'll say so."

### What "tryhard superhuman" looks like (AVOID):
- Metaphor-stuffed openers ("Picture this: a storm brews on the horizon of your sales funnel...")
- Rhetorical ladders that never land ("But what if I told you... what if there was a way... what if everything you knew was wrong?")
- Forced pattern interrupts on every line (gets exhausting, reads as desperate)
- Stylized formatting (ALL CAPS paragraphs, excessive italics, emoji overload)
- Drama without substance ("This changes everything.")

### What real superhuman looks like:
- Radical specificity
- Confident restraint (says it once, well)
- Natural rhythm
- One clear voice
- Earned CTAs (the reader already wanted the next step because the copy made it obvious)

The best copy doesn't announce itself as great. It just converts.

## FORMATTING RULES (NON-NEGOTIABLE)

These are the hard rules. Everything above is the craft. These are the guardrails.

### Universal
1. **No em dashes.** Ever. Use commas and periods.
2. **No voicemails.** Never generate voicemail scripts. Use RizzDial's `double_call: true` instead.
3. **No hashtags.** Ever.
4. **Contractions always.** "you're," "we'll," "can't," "don't."
5. **Mirror the client's language** from the offer doc. If they say "patients," you say "patients."
6. **Variables with fallbacks:** If `{{first_name}}` is empty, use "Hey there," not "Hey {{first_name}}."

### SMS
- Under 160 characters ideal. Over 320 forbidden.
- ONE CTA per text.
- No links in the first text of a sequence.
- Lowercase starts occasionally (roughly 1 in 4 messages).
- Fragments are good.
- Drop apostrophes sometimes in casual texts ("dont", "im", "youre").
- Never use semicolons in SMS. Ever.
- Never "Dear," "Hello," "Greetings," "I hope this finds you well."
- One emoji max per text, and only if the brand voice supports it. Default to zero.

### Email
- Under 200 words per email.
- ONE CTA.
- Subject line under 50 characters, curiosity-driven, never clickbait.
- Open with the hook, not "Hey {{first_name}}!"
- Short paragraphs. Mostly 1-3 lines.
- Scannable. If the reader can't grasp the idea in 5 seconds of skimming, it's too long.

### Chatbot (GHL Conversational AI)
- Responses under 3 sentences.
- 3-5% typo rate configured in GHL (rough rule: 1 in 20-30 messages has a minor typo).
- Reaction words before next question: "Got it!", "Makes sense," "For sure," "Nice."
- One question at a time.

### Voice Scripts (for reference — generated by /new-voice-ai-prompt, not this skill)
- Disfluencies woven INTO the script lines: "um," "uh," "like," "yeah," "gotcha," "I mean."
- Roughly 1 disfluency every 3-4 sentences.
- Short punchy sentences mixed with longer flowing ones.
- React before the next question.
- Never sounds like reading.

## VARIABLE USAGE

Standard GHL variables used throughout:
- `{{first_name}}` — contact first name (fallback: "Hey there")
- `{{last_name}}` — contact last name
- `{{phone}}` — contact phone
- `{{email}}` — contact email
- `{{booking_link}}` — calendar booking link
- `{{appointment_date_and_time}}` — confirmed appointment datetime
- `{{appointment_time}}` — just the time portion
- `{{current_dateTime}}` — for timezone-aware scripts

Rules:
- Never display raw variable names to the reader.
- Every variable needs a fallback or a conditional wrap.
- Variables mid-sentence must punctuate naturally ("{{first_name}}?" works; "{{first_name}}." looks off).

## THE 3-TEXT SMS PATTERN (the industry workhorse)

Highest-converting inbound SMS sequence. Use for every speed-to-lead workflow.

**Text 1** (fires 10-30s after opt-in):
```
Hey {{first_name}}, quick question about the [thing they signed up for]
```
Under 70 chars. No link. Builds curiosity. References what they opted in for.

**Text 2** (30s after Text 1):
```
This is [Agent] from [Company]. Before you [take the obvious next step], there's one thing you should know.
```
Identity + hook. Creates an open loop they want closed.

**Text 3** (30s after Text 2):
```
Got 15 min this week? I can pull your [profile/numbers/etc] and show you [specific outcome]: {{booking_link}}
```
The ask, framed as a value offer not a pitch. First link of the sequence.

**Text 4** (24 hours later, if no response):
```
{{first_name}}?
```
Single word. Question mark. Highest reply rate of any message in any sequence. Use it.

### Why this works:
- Staggered timing feels like a real person typing
- Short messages feel personal, not automated
- Text 1 builds intrigue without asking for anything
- Text 2 creates an open loop that demands closure
- Text 3 offers specific value, not a vague "chat"
- Text 4 at 24hr creates guilt + curiosity. The reader feels obligated to respond.

## THE FINAL GUT CHECK

Before any piece of copy ships, ask:

1. **Is there a real number, name, or scene in it?** If no, add one.
2. **Would I say this to a friend?** If no, rewrite.
3. **Is there one clear idea?** If it has two, cut one.
4. **Did I earn the CTA?** If the reader wouldn't naturally want the next step by the time they get there, the copy above it failed.
5. **Does the rhythm vary?** If every sentence is 12-15 words, rewrite three of them.
6. **Are there AI tells?** Search for em dashes, "leverage," "unlock," "transform," "navigate." Replace.
7. **Does it sound like a person or a brand?** Person wins.

If it passes all seven, ship it.
