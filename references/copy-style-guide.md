# Copy Style Guide -- Non-Negotiable Rules

> These rules apply to EVERY piece of copy generated. No exceptions.
> Violating any of these makes the copy sound AI-generated and kills conversion.

---

## THE CARDINAL RULE

**Every piece of copy must pass this test: "Would a real human send this?"**

If it sounds like a template, a bot, a corporate newsletter, or a marketing email, rewrite it. Real humans text casually. Real humans tell stories. Real humans don't use em dashes.

---

## FORMATTING RULES

### No Em Dashes
- NEVER use em dashes in SMS, email, voice scripts, or any client-facing copy
- Use commas and periods instead for pauses and breaks
- This is the #1 tell that copy is AI-generated

### SMS Rules (MUST SOUND LIKE A REAL HUMAN TEXTING)

THE TEST: Before writing any SMS, imagine a 28-year-old sales rep picking up their phone and texting a lead. Not a marketing department. Not a drip campaign. A person.

**Structure:**
- Under 160 characters ideally (one SMS segment)
- NEVER over 320 characters (two segments max)
- One CTA per text, not multiple asks
- No links in the first text (build curiosity first)

**Human-sounding rules (NON-NEGOTIABLE):**
- Occasionally drop a capital letter at the start. Maybe 1 in 5 messages starts lowercase. Real humans don't capitalize every text.
- Casual contractions: "gonna" not "going to", "wanna" not "want to", "kinda" not "kind of"
- Sentence fragments are GOOD. "Worth a look?" beats "Would this be worth looking into?"
- Questions feel more human than statements. "Still thinking about it?" beats "We wanted to follow up."
- Use periods sparingly in short texts. "Hey just checking in" feels more human than "Hey, just checking in."
- NEVER use semicolons in SMS. Nobody texts with semicolons.
- NEVER say "I wanted to reach out" or "per our conversation" or "at your earliest convenience"
- NEVER use "regarding", "pertaining", "inquire", "utilize", or "leverage"
- No bullet points in SMS. Nobody texts in bullet points.
- No "Dear" or "Hello" or "Greetings". Always "Hey" or just start talking.
- No hashtags. No excessive emojis (one max, only if brand supports it).

**Spelling error rule (for GHL Conversational AI / chatbot ONLY):**
- Configure 3-5% typo rate in the chatbot. Roughly 1 in 20 messages has a minor typo.
- Example: "Waht time works best?" or "Thats awesome let me grab you a time"
- DO NOT put typos in the pre-written SMS templates (client reviews those). Typo rate is for live chatbot responses only.
- In templates, use informality instead: drop capitals, fragments, casual abbreviations.

**What AI-written SMS looks like (NEVER DO THIS):**
- "Hello {{first_name}}, I hope this message finds you well. I wanted to reach out regarding the funding opportunity you expressed interest in."
- "Hi {{first_name}}! We're excited to connect with you about your goals. Our team of experts is ready to help you maximize your potential."
- "Dear {{first_name}}, Thank you for your interest in our services."

**What human-written SMS looks like (ALWAYS DO THIS):**
- "hey {{first_name}}, quick question about the funding you signed up for"
- "this is Blake from Jeff's team. before you apply anywhere theres one thing you should know"
- "{{first_name}}?"
- "not trying to blow you up. just dont want you to apply somewhere and mess up your profile before we can help"
- "real talk, most people with a 700+ score are sitting on 50-150k and dont even know it"

Notice: lowercase starts, dropped apostrophes sometimes, fragments, casual language, zero corporate speak.

### Email Rules
- Under 200 words per email
- One CTA per email
- Short paragraphs (1-3 sentences each)
- Subject lines under 50 characters, curiosity-driven, no clickbait
- No "Hey {{first_name}}!" as the opener (everyone does this, it screams automation)
- Open with the story or hook, not the greeting
- Scannable. If they can't get the point in 5 seconds of skimming, it's too long.

### Voice Script Rules
- Natural disfluencies woven INTO the spoken lines: "um," "uh," "like," "yeah," "you know," "gotcha," "I mean"
- Roughly 1 disfluency every 3-4 sentences
- Always contractions: "you're," "they're," "gonna," "wanna," "gotta"
- Short punchy sentences mixed with longer flowing ones
- React before next question: "I hear you." "That's real." "Yeah, I get that."
- Pause instructions after every question
- Never sounds like reading

### Voicemail Rules
- Under 30 seconds
- Identify yourself and reason in first 3 seconds
- One clear next step
- Callback number at the end (once, clearly)
- Sound confident, not desperate
- Conversational, not scripted

## TONE AND VOICE

### What It Sounds Like
- A smart friend who happens to know your industry really well
- Warm but direct. Cares about you but doesn't coddle you
- Confident without being arrogant
- Casual without being unprofessional
- Like the best rep they've ever talked to

### What It Does NOT Sound Like
- A marketing department
- A corporate newsletter
- A chatbot reading a FAQ
- A desperate salesperson
- A generic template with [FIRST_NAME] plugged in

### Word Choices
- "Hey" not "Hello" or "Dear"
- "Quick question" not "I wanted to reach out regarding"
- "This is [name] from [company]" not "On behalf of [company], I am writing to"
- "Book a time" not "Schedule a consultation at your earliest convenience"
- "What's going on?" not "Could you please describe your current situation?"
- "That's rough" not "I understand that must be challenging"

## SALES PSYCHOLOGY (BAKED INTO EVERY PIECE)

### Pattern Interrupts
- Every first touch must say something unexpected
- Never start with generic greetings that let the brain auto-reject
- The first 7 seconds determine if they keep reading or listening

### Loss Aversion
- Frame as what they're LOSING, not what they'd gain
- "80% of your leads are dying in your inbox" beats "we can help you book more appointments"
- People are 2x more motivated to avoid loss than to gain something

### Permission Closes
- Get micro-yeses throughout: "Sound good?" "Fair enough?" "Make sense?"
- Each yes makes the final yes easier
- Minimum 3 permission closes per voice call

### The Assumptive Bridge
- Never ask IF they want to proceed. Ask WHEN or HOW
- "So the next step is getting you on the calendar" not "Would you like to schedule?"
- "I'm gonna send you the link" not "Would you like me to send the link?"

### Social Proof
- Reference specific results: "$44/week cost per lead" not "great results"
- Name drops when relevant: Krista Mashore, Les Brown, Sheldon Ho
- "We've done this for hundreds of businesses" not "we help businesses"

### Urgency (Real, Not Fake)
- Capacity-based: "We're only onboarding 5 this month"
- Time-based: "Your competitors are already using this"
- Cost-based: "Every day without this costs you $X"
- Never fake scarcity. If it's not real, don't say it.

## THE 3-TEXT SMS PATTERN

This is the highest-converting inbound SMS sequence. Use for every speed-to-lead workflow.

**Text 1:** "Hey {{first_name}}" (wait 30 seconds)
**Text 2:** "This is [agent] from [company]" (wait 30 seconds)
**Text 3:** "Book a time with us here: {{booking_link}}"

Then 24 hours later:
**Text 4:** "{{first_name}}?" (first name only with question mark)

This single-word follow-up consistently produces the highest reply rate of any message.

### Why It Works:
- Staggered timing makes it feel like someone actually typing
- Short messages feel personal, not automated
- The 24hr first-name-only text triggers curiosity and forces a response
- It's so simple that it can't be ignored

## SEINFELD EMAIL METHOD

Named after how Seinfeld episodes seem random but always connect.

### Structure Per Email:
1. **Hook** (1-2 sentences) -- Start with a story, observation, or unexpected statement
2. **Story/Body** (3-5 sentences) -- Connect the story to their situation
3. **Bridge** (1 sentence) -- Link to the offer/CTA naturally
4. **Soft CTA** (1 sentence) -- Casual, no pressure, woven into the narrative

### Rules:
- Tell a short story (real or hypothetical)
- Connect it to the prospect's pain or desire
- End with a casual CTA, not a hard pitch
- Be entertaining enough they WANT to open the next one
- Never feel like a template
- Leave open loops to drive next email opens
- No "unsubscribe" footer language in the copy itself (that's automated)

### What Kills Seinfeld Emails:
- Corporate language ("We're excited to announce...")
- No personality
- Hard CTAs ("Click here now! Limited time!")
- No story element
- Feeling templated
- Too many paragraphs
- No callback to previous emails

## VARIABLE USAGE

### Standard Variables (GHL):
- `{{first_name}}` -- contact first name
- `{{last_name}}` -- contact last name
- `{{phone}}` -- contact phone
- `{{email}}` -- contact email
- `{{booking_link}}` -- calendar booking link
- `{{appointment_date_and_time}}` -- confirmed appointment datetime
- `{{current_dateTime}}` -- current date/time (for timezone-aware scripts)

### Rules:
- If `{{first_name}}` is empty, use "Hey there" not "Hey {{first_name}}"
- Never display raw variable names in any copy
- Always have a fallback for empty variables
